Overview

The process changed, and so did the role.

In 2021, Nipissing Township set out to hire a CAO-Clerk-Deputy Treasurer. Instead, the process shifted midstream. The role was redefined as Municipal Administrator, and the person already serving in the acting position was appointed into that new structure.

What happened

How the file moved

1

The Township launched a recruitment process

An RFP was issued to hire a consultant to help recruit a CAO-Clerk-Deputy Treasurer.

2

The acting official stayed in the middle of the process

Submissions were directed to the Acting CAO-Clerk-Treasurer, who remained in place while the process unfolded.

3

The structure changed midstream

Instead of completing the recruitment under the original model, the consultant recommended a different structure built around a Municipal Administrator role.

4

Council approved the new structure

Three by-laws followed: one for Municipal Administrator, one for Clerk, and one for Treasurer.

What doesn’t add up

Why this weakens confidence

Issue 01

The Township started one process and finished another

The Township did not set out to create a Municipal Administrator role. It set out to hire a CAO. Changing the structure during the process made the outcome feel less independent.

Issue 02

The same person stayed close to the file throughout

Even if each step was technically valid, the optics are difficult: the acting official received submissions, stayed in place during the process, and then ended up in the new model.

Issue 03

The paperwork made the optics worse

Residents were left with a simple question: how does someone sign as Municipal Administrator on the same by-law that appoints them to that role? Even if legally fine, it creates confusion.

The real issue

Trust starts with a process people can follow.

This case is not mainly about whether the final structure could work. It is about whether residents could clearly see why the process changed, who benefited from that change, and why Council did not separate structure from hiring more cleanly.

The problem is not just the outcome.

It is that the route to the outcome was never explained clearly enough to build public confidence.

What I’d do differently

Three simple standards

A

Separate structure from hiring

If a consultant recommends a different model, Council should pause, debate the structure publicly, and only then restart recruitment under the new model.

B

Keep the process visibly independent

No acting official should sit so close to a process that determines their own future role.

C

Explain the change to the public

Residents should have heard clearly what changed, why it changed, and what the Township believed it was gaining.

What this points to

The issue is bigger than one file.

This case is one example of why Nipissing Township needs stronger oversight, clearer communication, and decisions that are easier for residents to follow from start to finish.
