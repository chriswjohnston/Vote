CAO Recruitment — The Process Changed, and So Did the Role

Nipissing Township set out to hire a CAO-Clerk-Deputy Treasurer. Instead, the process changed midstream, the role was redefined, and the acting official inside the organization was appointed into the new structure.

What changed RFP for a CAO-Clerk-Deputy Treasurer ended with a Municipal Administrator appointment
What concerns me The process changed midstream and the acting official remained inside it
What this shows When structure, hiring, and oversight blur together, confidence breaks down
What happened

One process went in. A different outcome came out.

May 2021

RFP issued

The Township issues an RFP to manage a search for a CAO-Clerk-Deputy Treasurer.

June 2021

Consultant hired

Council accepts a $9,315 proposal from e4M to run the recruitment process.

Sept 2021

Role changes midstream

Council is advised to shift from CAO to a Municipal Administrator model.

Sept 2021

Appointment made

The acting CAO-Clerk-Treasurer is appointed into the new structure.

The key issue

The acting official was inside the process from the start.

The RFP directed submissions to the Acting CAO-Clerk-Treasurer — meaning the person ultimately appointed was directly connected to the process from the beginning.

Even if technically allowed, this creates a serious optics problem.
What doesn’t add up

Three reasons this weakens confidence

Issue 01

Different process than promised

The Township started a CAO recruitment and ended with a different role entirely.

Issue 02

Process proximity

The same individual remained close to the process throughout.

Issue 03

By-law optics

The appointment by-law is signed by the person being appointed using the title they are being given.

This is what residents notice.

If a document appoints someone to a role and is signed by that same person using that role, it raises obvious questions.

Independent review

This didn’t stop at Council.

After reviewing the process, I formally raised concerns. That led to an investigation by the Ontario Ombudsman.

The Ombudsman found that Council violated the Municipal Act by discussing its hiring plan in closed session.

Hiring senior leadership — including structure and process — should be handled openly. Residents have a right to understand how those decisions are made.

What this shows

Oversight before office

I raised concerns and pursued them formally, even without a seat at Council.

Why it matters

Transparency matters most here

Leadership decisions shape the Township for years. They must be handled properly.

Read the Ombudsman summary →

What I’d do differently

Three simple standards

A

Separate structure from hiring

Decide the structure first, then run a clean hiring process.

B

Ensure independence

No candidate should sit inside their own hiring process.

C

Explain decisions publicly

Residents should understand what changed and why.
