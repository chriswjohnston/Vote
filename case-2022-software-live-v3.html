Municipal Software Replacement — Four Years to Get There

The Township approved a new software path in 2022. The original route did not land, the project drifted for years, and a working system only arrived after a vendor change and a reset.

Why it matters

Flawed procurementScope driftLate resetWeak oversight

A major systems project should not drift for years without a clear public reset. Even when the final outcome works, residents should be able to follow how the Township got there.

What concerns meThere is no clear point in the public record where Council openly said the process was off track and needed a reset.

Overview

Four years to get to a system that worked.

In 2022, the Township approved a new municipal software path. Years later, the original vendor direction had failed, funds had continued to move, and a working outcome only arrived after the Township switched vendors and effectively restarted.

What happened

How the file drifted

1

An RFP was issued

Two submissions came in: MuniSoft at roughly $45,741 and TownSuite at roughly $411,150.

2

The RFP was declared inadequate

Instead of restarting the process, the Township moved toward a hybrid solution involving TownSuite plus other systems.

3

The project drifted for years

Funds carried forward, implementation did not land cleanly, and there was no visible public reset when the path was clearly failing.

4

The Township switched vendors

By 2025, Council acknowledged TownSuite had not converted successfully for Township needs. The agreement was terminated and MuniSoft was approved.

What doesn’t add up

The process never really reset

Issue 01

The RFP was called inadequate — but the process kept going

If the Township could not rely on the RFP to determine the required system, that should have triggered a reset, not a workaround.

Issue 02

The final path did not match the original competition

The Township did not simply choose either submission. The scope and solution changed, but the procurement process did not visibly restart.

Issue 03

Years passed without a clear public course correction

Residents can see the drift in the record. What is hard to see is the moment Council said clearly enough: this is not working, and we need a different path.

The real issue

What did the Township actually buy?

Municipal software is not one product. It is modules, add-ons, integrations, and configuration choices. Without a clearer public record of what was purchased, what failed, and what was ultimately delivered, it is hard for residents to know whether the Township got value.

A working outcome does not erase a weak process.

The Township now has a functioning system. The concern is how many years of drift it took to get there, and how little visible accountability showed up along the way.

What I’d do differently

What better oversight would look like

A

Restart a flawed procurement process

If the RFP no longer reflects the actual solution, Council should stop and restart instead of letting a major purchase drift into a different scope.

B

Demand milestone reporting

Council should receive clear, recurring progress checkpoints on major implementations with visible go/no-go moments.

C

Keep a public record of what was delivered

Residents should be able to see the scope, modules, and final outcome of a project this significant.

What this points to

The issue is bigger than one file.

This case is one example of why Nipissing Township needs stronger oversight, clearer communication, and decisions that are easier for residents to follow from start to finish.
