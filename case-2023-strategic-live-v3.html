Strategic Plan — Too Short to Be Strategic

Every Township needs a plan that actually guides decisions. Nipissing Township’s current plan was short, approved late, and timed in a way that creates another awkward transition during the next election cycle.

Why it matters

Short horizonLate approvalWeak continuityElection-year reset

A strategic plan should create continuity, outlast the Council that wrote it, and shape major decisions. When it does not, the Township is patching from term to term instead of planning ahead.

What changedThe previous plan still had time left when the Township began another process, and the new plan was approved late enough that part of its own timeline had already passed.
What concerns meThe planning horizon is too short and too tied to election timing to provide strong long-term direction.
What this showsA plan that is too short or too late stops guiding decisions and starts functioning more like a document than a strategy.
Overview

Too short to be strategic.

The Township’s previous plan covered 2019–2024. Its next plan covered 2023–2026 and was approved late in 2023. That means the new plan overlapped the old one, started partly in the past, and ends during another election year.

What happened

A short plan built late in the cycle

1

The Township already had a plan

The previous strategic plan ran from 2019 to 2024.

2

A new planning process started before the old one expired

In 2023, Council began work on another plan instead of using the remaining runway of the previous one.

3

The new plan covered 2023–2026

Approved late in 2023, it effectively started after part of its own timeline had already passed.

4

The next Council inherits another awkward transition

Because the plan ends during an election year, the Township risks another gap or another short-term patch.

What doesn’t add up

Why the timeline matters

Issue 01

The plan is too short to guide long-term decisions

Strategic planning should look beyond one Council term. A short plan approved late in the cycle does not do that well.

Issue 02

The timing creates continuity problems

Either the next Council inherits a gap, or planning restarts again during an election year. Neither outcome reflects strong long-term direction.

Issue 03

The process did not build enough real direction

Residents were invited into the process, but that is different from giving them meaningful influence over the direction of the final plan.

The real issue

A strategic plan should outlast the Council that wrote it.

If a plan is too short, too late, or too disconnected from actual decision-making, it stops functioning as strategy and starts functioning as branding. A Township plan should guide hiring, procurement, technology, and capital priorities over time.

Planning is supposed to create continuity.

When planning repeatedly bends around election timing instead of looking beyond it, Council is reacting to the term instead of shaping the future.

What I’d do differently

A better planning standard

A

Use a longer planning horizon

A strategic plan should cover 5–10 years, not just edge up against the next election cycle.

B

Adopt it early in the term

The plan should be ready soon enough to guide the Council’s major decisions, not appear after part of the timeline has already passed.

C

Make it the frame for major decisions

If hiring, procurement, and capital choices are not tied back to the plan, then the plan is not doing real work.

What this points to

The issue is bigger than one file.

This case is one example of why Nipissing Township needs stronger oversight, clearer communication, and decisions that are easier for residents to follow from start to finish.
